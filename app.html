Penalty Weights
The algorithm starts with a base score of 5,000 and deducts points for every rule violation. It then tries to maximize this score. A perfect timetable scores 5,000.
200
Higher = algorithm avoids hard violations more aggressively. Example: with 200, a single teacher conflict costs −200 points.
15
Lower = soft rules are more relaxed (e.g. subject placement preference). Raise this to enforce soft rules more strictly.
Recommended ratio
Hard penalty should be 10–20× the soft penalty so the algorithm always fixes hard violations first before optimizing soft ones. Default 200 / 15 ≈ 13×.

Algorithm
🧬
Genetic
Population-based evolutionary search with crossover & mutation across all classes
🌡️
Simulated Annealing
Probabilistic hill-climbing that escapes local optima via temperature cooling
🔐
Constraint Prog.
Backtracking search with forward-checking for guaranteed constraint satisfaction
🧬 The Genetic Algorithm mimics natural selection — it evolves a population of candidate timetables over many generations, combining the best ones (crossover) and occasionally making small random changes (mutation) to find an optimal solution.
40
How many different timetable drafts the algorithm works with simultaneously each generation. Think of it as how many "attempts" compete against each other at once.
↑ Higher = explores more solutions, better quality — but slower.
↓ Lower = faster but may miss better solutions.
Recommended: 30–60 for most schools.
150
How many evolutionary cycles to run. Each generation, the worst timetables are discarded and replaced with new ones bred from the best.
↑ More generations = more refinement, higher fitness — but takes longer.
↓ Fewer = quick result, less polished.
Recommended: 100–200 for good results.
8%
The chance that a random period swap is applied to a timetable after breeding. Prevents the algorithm from getting stuck on a "good enough" schedule by introducing variety.
↑ Higher = more exploration, escapes local traps — but too high makes it chaotic (random search).
↓ Lower = more stable convergence — but risks getting stuck.
Recommended: 5–12% for typical schedules.
15%
The top % of timetables that are carried over unchanged to the next generation, guaranteeing the best solutions are never lost.
↑ Higher = best solutions are protected longer, steady improvement.
↓ Lower = more exploration but risks losing good solutions.
Recommended: 10–20%. At 15%, the top 6 out of 40 timetables survive each cycle.
🌡️ Simulated Annealing works like a cooling metal — it starts "hot" (willing to accept worse solutions to escape traps) and gradually "cools" (becoming more selective). A single timetable is refined step-by-step rather than evolving a whole population. Faster than GA but can get stuck on complex schools.
5000
Controls how willing the algorithm is to accept a worse solution at the start. At high temperature, even significantly worse timetables are accepted — this lets it escape from local traps early on.
↑ Higher = more exploration at the start, better at escaping bad starting points — but takes longer to settle.
↓ Lower = starts conservative, faster but may get stuck.
Recommended: 3,000–8,000 for most school sizes.
0.96
How quickly the temperature drops each step. Temperature is multiplied by this value every iteration — so 0.96 means temperature shrinks by 4% each step.
↑ Closer to 1.0 (e.g. 0.99) = cools slowly, more exploration, better quality — but needs more iterations to finish.
↓ Lower (e.g. 0.90) = cools fast, finishes quickly — but may freeze before finding a good solution.
Recommended: 0.95–0.97. At 0.96 with 2,000 iterations, temperature drops from 5,000 to ~≈5.
2000
Total number of swap attempts to make. Each iteration randomly swaps two periods in the current timetable and decides whether to keep or discard the change.
↑ More iterations = more refinement steps, higher quality — directly proportional to runtime.
↓ Fewer = faster, but the temperature may still be too high when it stops (not fully settled).
Recommended: match to cooling rate — enough iterations for temperature to reach near zero. 2,000 pairs well with 0.96.
🔐 Constraint Programming starts with a random schedule, then systematically detects rule violations and tries to fix them by swapping conflicting periods to empty slots. It's the fastest algorithm but the least flexible — it fixes what it can find rather than exploring creative solutions. Best used when you need a quick draft.
2000
The maximum number of conflict-fixing moves the algorithm is allowed to make. Each time a teacher conflict is detected, it tries to move one of the clashing periods to a free slot — that counts as one backtrack.
↑ Higher = more attempts to resolve conflicts, better chance of reaching 0 hard violations.
↓ Lower = stops sooner, faster but may leave hard violations unresolved.
Recommended: 2,000–5,000. Increase if Hard Viols. stays above 0 after generation.
Determines which conflict to tackle first when multiple violations exist simultaneously.
MRV (Minimum Remaining Values) — picks the most constrained teacher first (fewest free slots). Best default choice.
Degree — picks the teacher involved in the most conflicts. Good for dense schedules with many shared teachers.
Random — picks randomly. Useful to compare against the others or break ties differently.
